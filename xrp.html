History of XRP

XRP was created by three engineers—David Schwartz, Jed McCaleb, and Arthur Britto—in 2011. They aimed to develop a more efficient and sustainable digital asset compared to Bitcoin, specifically designed for payments. Their work led to the creation of the XRP Ledger (XRPL), which officially launched in June 2012. Initially, the project was called Ripple, and the digital asset was referred to as "ripples" (XRP). The founders gifted 80 billion XRP to a new company, which was initially named NewCoin, then OpenCoin, and eventually rebranded to Ripple Labs. Chris Larsen joined as CEO, and the team focused on building use cases for XRP within the financial system. Unlike Bitcoin, which relies on a proof-of-work consensus mechanism, the XRP Ledger uses a consensus protocol based on the Federated Byzantine Agreement (FBA). This allows for faster and more energy-efficient transactions, with the ledger capable of handling thousands of transactions per second.

Prediction of XRP in 2030

XRP has been a significant player in the cryptocurrency market, particularly known for its use in facilitating cross-border payments. By 2030, it is expected to continue its growth, driven by its utility and adoption in the financial sector. Predictions for XRP's price in 2030 vary widely. Some analysts forecast that XRP could reach between $5 and $15, depending on market conditions and adoption. More conservative estimates suggest a price around $6.75, while others are more optimistic, predicting it could go as high as $10 to $15.

Market Supply of XRP

As of now, the circulating supply of XRP is approximately 56.1 billion tokens. This represents the number of XRP tokens currently available and actively traded in the market. The total supply of XRP is around 100 billion tokens. This includes all XRP tokens that have been created, including those that are not yet in circulation. XRP's market capitalization is approximately $32.3 billion. This metric reflects the total market value of all circulating XRP tokens.
